The Patent War

Patent 821,393, filed by Wrights, March of 1903.
“In Hammondsport, the old-timers used to say that if you jumped up in the air and flapped your arms you’d be infringing on the Wrights’ patent.” [12]
After the success of the AEA, the Wrights were scared that they would soon be forced out of business by competitors. They decided to sue other aviators for infringing upon their patent held on aircraft.
Hammondsport Herald's Announcement of Wright Brothers' "Claim [of] Infringement of Patents," August 25, 1909.
Wright Diagram of Wing Warping Kite, 1899.
Current Day Aileron Diagram, n.d.
“They went into a lengthy patent battle for a long period of time, so the Wright Brothers wanted to patent the aircraft, and anyone who built an aircraft had to pay a big license, so this Patent Battle went on for a quite a period of time.” [13]
The Wrights eventually won the battle, with a decision by the Second Circuit court of Appeals. But not only did this Patent Battle waste the time and money of Curtiss and the Wrights, but it also limited the production of aircraft both in the US and in foreign countries.
[12] Seth Shulman, Unlocking the Sky: Glenn Hammond Curtiss and the Race to Invent the Airplane (New York: HarperCollins Publishers, 2002), 39.
[13] Scott, Interview by author.
