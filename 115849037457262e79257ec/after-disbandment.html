After Disbandment

Curtiss No.1 Golden Flyer, 1909.
Patent 1246011, Curtiss Flying Boat, filed by Curtiss Co., pg 1, 1917.
Patent 1246011, Curtiss Flying Boat, filed by Curtiss Co., pg 2, 1917.
Curtiss NC-4, 1919.
Curtiss would go on to design aircraft that would dazzle thousands in exhibitions, proving to the public the viability of flight. He devoted himself to inventing the flying boat, a craft that could both takeoff and land on water.
"I had been told that the Secretary of the Navy regarded the adaptability of the aeroplane to navy uses as depending very largely on its ability to alight on the water and be hoisted aboard a warship. With the hydroaeroplane I had developed, I had no doubts about being able to do this, without any platform or preparation on board the vessel." [11]

​After experimenting with having the June Bug take off from water, the Association disbanded in the March of 1909. Curtiss’ first post-Association aircraft was the Golden Flier, powered by a new engine that, in the words of Curtiss, “[developed] more power per square inch of piston area than has ever been secured in a gas engine.” ​ [10]
Curtiss went on to use a modified Golden Flier in the 1909 Gordon Bennett Cup, a speed trial in Reims, France, and won by six seconds. In the aftermath, Curtiss exhibited his aircraft throughout Europe to great acclaim.
[10] Goldstone, Birdmen, 149.
[11] Curtiss, The Curtiss Aviation Book, 132-133.
