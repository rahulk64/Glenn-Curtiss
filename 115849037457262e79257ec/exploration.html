Exploration

An aircraft 'rolling', or 'banking', with its ailerons, n.d.
Curtiss made large contributions to aviation by exploring the possibilities of what could be done. Curtiss aircraft were equipped with ailerons, which demonstrated superior control in flight, and he worked tirelessly to invent the flying boat and hydroplanes. ​
“[Ailerons were] probably the biggest [exploration]. Just about every aircraft on the planet uses ailerons, or some type of articulating flight control... it very quickly became apparent that if you didn’t have ailerons, you were in trouble. In fact, there was an aircraft, the German aircraft, called the Taube, it was the last wing-warping aircraft, and in the start of World War I, it was so heavily out-maneuvered, that it suffered tragic losses from aircraft that had ailerons.” [14]
Marshall Earle Reid at Keuka Lake in his Curtiss seaplane, ca. 1912.

Curtiss believed in the future of aviation as evidenced by his saying, “An immediate future for commercial flying is assured. It is assured for the reason that aeroplanes are already finding work to do in a world of peace. The crossing of the Atlantic by the Navy-Curtiss planes has simply been the most spectacular of a number of achievements. Aeroplanes are daily delivering mail between New York and Washington.” [15] He was an innovator who always looked forward.
[14] Scott, Interview by author.
[15] Glenn Hammond Curtiss, “The Aviation Outlook,” Aeronautics (1919): 649.
