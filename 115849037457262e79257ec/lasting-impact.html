Lasting Impact

An Image of the USS Enterprise (CV-6) with tons of aircraft on it, ca. 1939.
“Changes have come so fast in the development of the aeroplane that what is new today may well be tomorrow, but since we have kept in intimate touch with all the foreign designs and developments, I have full confidence in the ability of American manufacturers to make fighting planes...” [20]
Naval aviation was instrumental in the Pacific Theater of the second World War, helping the allies secure their victory over Japan. [21]
“[Curtiss], as a famous person, got to promote aviation, both to the military, and to a lesser extent the civilian world, which allowed his company to grow, and admit that you could have this very large aerospace company, rather than a small one here and a small one here and here. He helped create the idea of a large aerospace corporation.” [22]
Curtiss P-40 Planes, 1943.
Deck of the USS Lexington (CV-2), 1929.
Western Front Air Combat in World War I, from the Air Service's United States Army Collection, 1978.
An F-117 Nighthawk stealth strike aircraft flying over Nevada, August 2002.
“When the perilous days were past he devoted his time to the building up of the aircraft industry, and established one of the two biggest firms of aircraft constructors in the United States.” [23]
Curtiss-Wright Company Logo, 2014.
Boeing 777 in flight, ca. 1992.
The American aviation industry continued to grow after Curtiss left the industry. The Curtiss and Wright companies merged into the Curtiss-Wright Co. Corporations such as Boeing and Lockheed Martin continue to be at the forefront of the aerospace industry, and we wouldn’t be at the level we are today without Curtiss.
