The Association
Dr. Bell brought Curtiss, along with Canadian engineers Frederick “Casey” Baldwin, John McCurdy, and a U.S. Army Lieutenant, Thomas Selfridge. At first, the group experimented with kites, but the young group was not interested in pursuing kites for very long, and voted to move the operation to Hammondsport to acquire the motors produced by the Curtiss factory. There, the group worked on Selfridge’s design for an airplane.
“Up until that point in time, the Wright Brothers used wing warping, which is how a bird flies, [which] changes the shape of the wing. It’s a very difficult thing to do, in fact NASA only now is trying to get back into that area of using smart materials to change the shape of the wings, so that you can have different shaped wings, slow speed coming into land, and an airliner could have a smoother wing and high speed travel. So, he took the rather difficult flight controls used by the Wright Brothers and decided to articulate the ailerons in such a way that he could [better] control his aircraft... [and created a more] stable aircraft, but the stable aircraft would also have what they called maneuverability, so the ability to maneuver the aircraft a lot more effectively.” [8]

"The Winning Flight of the "June Bug" Aeroplane for the Scientific American Trophy," July 18, 1908.
The White Wing, the first plane to adopt ailerons, was a success, and the data recorded from its tests led to the June Bug. And in July of 1908, Curtiss brought the June Bug out to try for the Scientific American trophy, an award for the first public flight of over one kilometer. 
“The big crowd waited for the machine to be hauled back to the track and at about 7:30 the June Bug again soared into the air and easily and gracefully made the required kilometer and sailed on covering in all a mile and a quarter. The alighting was accomplished without accident. The cheering of the crowd could be heard for miles, and Mr. Curtiss was overwhelmed with congratulations as soon as the people could reach him. The Scientific American trophy which Mr. Curtiss won is valued at $2,500. It was presented' by the Scientific American to the Aero Club of America for the purpose of developing the art of aviation...” [9]
