Exchange

Curtiss V-8 Engine, 1906.
​While “[Curtiss] and the Wright Brothers didn’t exchange ideas,” [16] there was still a large-scale exchange of ideas. While the Wright Brothers kept their inventions a secret, there was the exchange of motors with Baldwin and Bell, and there was an open exchange of ideas in the aviation world. Curtiss workers openly exchanged ideas in the shop for modifications and improvements to the motor [17], and Curtiss with the AEA exchanged ideas to develop better aircraft.
But there was not only an exchange of motors and ideas among individuals. He exchanged his aircraft (especially his flying boats) with not only the US Navy, but also many foreign countries.
​“[Curtiss] pretty much is the father of naval aviation... Eugene Ely, he was one of the display pilots for the Curtiss Aircraft Company, demonstrated that you could take off and land on a ship in a harbor, which meant you could do it at sea, so, it pretty much revolutionized the capabilities of any warship, any warship with a fixed-wing aircraft, mechanical, or heavier-than-air aircraft, could now expand the sensor, or the footprint, if you will, of the sensors of that warship or that battlegroup... but the idea was that Ely proved the somewhat reluctant navy that you could use an aircraft at sea, which had not been done.” [18]

“The Naval Authorities of France, Italy, Austria, England, and Russia have found an immediate use for [the flying boat] for patrolling their exposed coasts; and if their battleships are to be equipped with hydroaeroplanes this year, it is due greatly to the demonstrations given by the Curtiss machines.” [19]
[16] Scott, Interview by author.
[17] Shulman, Unlocking the Sky, 31.
[18] Scott, Interview by author.
[19] “The Aero Club of America Trophy for 1913-Awarded to Glenn H. Curtiss for the Development and Demonstration of the Flying Boat,” Aero Club of America (1913): 20.
